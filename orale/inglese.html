01

The Magnetic Compass

Variation & Deviation
📖 3 sezioni
🎴 7 flashcard
❓ 4 domande esame
🧲 Unit 8.1–8.2
📌 Unit 8 — Navigational aids and instruments
Il compasso magnetico è il più antico strumento di rotta. Per usarlo bene il navigatore deve correggere due errori: la variation (errore terrestre) e la deviation (errore della nave).

The Earth is like a giant magnet that generates a huge magnetic field having north and south magnetic poles. The magnetic needle of a compass always points to the magnetic north pole because it is affected by the Earth's magnetic field.

However, compass north does not exactly coincide with geographic (or true) north, which is the point where all meridians converge. The angle formed between the direction of magnetic north and the direction of true north is called magnetic variation (or declination), indicated with d in charts.

Magnetic variation varies both from place to place on the Earth's surface and with the passage of time (it can change as much as 2–25° every hundred years). Its value is printed on nautical charts, with details of the annual increase or decrease for the area.

The needle of a magnetic compass on board can be deviated from magnetic north because the ferrous metals in the ship's hull create disturbing magnetic fields. The compass needle can be deviated to the right or left from magnetic north by a certain angle (δ, delta) known as magnetic deviation.

Specialised technicians correct this deviation by placing magnets near the compass to compensate the ship's magnetic fields — but the effect can never be completely eliminated. The residual deviation is recorded in a deviation table, where the values are given for every 15° of all possible ship's headings, starting from 000°.

The Gyrocompass

True North · No magnetism
📖 2 sezioni
🎴 6 flashcard
❓ 3 domande esame
🌀 Unit 8.3

As long ago as the 15th century mariners noticed that the magnetic compass needle does not point accurately to true north (Columbus was aware of this on his voyages across the Atlantic in the 1490s).

The gyrocompass solved the problem because it indicates true north instead of magnetic north. The compass is set on a gyroscope, a fast spinning wheel powered by an electric motor. Once the gyroscope is rotating, it interacts with the force produced by the Earth's rotation and aligns its own spin axis with the rotational axis of the Earth. The electric motor keeps the gyroscope spinning, so the gyrocompass continues pointing in the same direction regardless of the ship's motion, even in rough seas.

On most modern ships the GPS or other navigational aids feed data to the gyrocompass, allowing a small computer to apply corrections.

The Sextant

Celestial navigation
📖 2 sezioni
🎴 6 flashcard
❓ 3 domande esame
📐 Unit 8.4

The sextant is an optical instrument invented in the middle of the 16th century, which measures the angle between two objects. The name means "the sixth part of a circle" because the scale of a sextant is equivalent to 60 degrees — one sixth of a circle.

Its primary use is to measure the angle, called altitude, between a celestial object and the horizon. This measurement is known as taking a sight (or sighting) and is an essential part of celestial navigation. The altitude angle is used to calculate the ship's position line on a nautical chart.

To measure the altitude of a celestial body over the horizon, you hold the instrument vertically in your right hand and make a sighting through the telescope. The horizon is seen in the horizon glass, while the celestial object is found in the mirror and positioned in line with the horizon. The arc shows the degrees, while the drum shows the minutes and seconds.

The sextant can also fix the ship's position by measuring the angle between two or three landmarks; in this case it is held horizontally.

Log · Echo Sounder · Sonar

Speed & Depth
📖 3 sezioni
🎴 6 flashcard
❓ 3 domande esame
📡 Unit 8.5 & 8.7

The log (solcometer) is used to measure the speed of a ship and the distance she has travelled. It is worked by a propeller or a rotor blade positioned under the hull. Distances and speed are calculated electronically and shown as numbers on screens placed above the charting table or near the Navigation Officer. The log can be connected to an electronic navigating system, so all information is constantly updated.

Sonar and echo sounders work in a similar way to radar, by sending a narrow range of sound waves towards the bottom of the sea. Both instruments are based on the principle that water is an excellent medium for the transmission of sound waves and that a sound pulse will "bounce off" when hitting a reflecting (solid) object, returning to its source as an echo.

They measure the distance from the reflecting object based on the speed of sound in water, which is around 1,500 metres per second (more than four times the speed of sound through air).

The word SONAR is an acronym for SOund NAvigation and Ranging. It was developed by the allied forces during the Second World War to track enemy submarines.

Echo sounders work on the principle of sonar: a sound pulse is emitted from the bottom of the hull, it bounces back from the seafloor and is picked up by the receiver. Since sound travels at a known rate, the sea depth can be calculated. A hard rocky bottom gives a stronger reflection than the softer tissue of fish, so echo sounders are often used on fishing boats to detect shoals of fish.

Radar & COLREGs

Radio waves · Anti-collision
📖 3 sezioni
🎴 7 flashcard
❓ 4 domande esame
📡 Unit 8.6

Radar works on the principle that every solid surface reflects radio waves: the waves "bounce back" from solid surfaces and their echo is picked up. The concept of using radio waves to detect objects goes back to 1902, but the practical system was developed during the Second World War by British inventors to detect moving objects coming towards the coastline of England and German planes. This purpose led to the acronym RADAR — RAdio Detection And Ranging.

Radio waves travel at the speed of light (300,000 km/s). Knowing this, we can measure the distance of detected objects. Modern radars use microwave radio frequencies (very short wavelengths, from 30 cm down to 1 cm), because they can be directed in narrow beams.

A marine radar has an antenna that both transmits and receives. The antenna constantly rotates, emitting short repeated pulses of microwave energy. When a beam strikes a solid object, part of the energy is reflected back (the echo) and picked up by the antenna. By calculating the time elapsed between the emission and the return of the pulse, we find the distance of the target.

The radar screen is called a PPI (Plan Position Indicator). It gives a representation of the area around the ship on all 360° of the horizon, extending as far as hundreds of miles. The ship appears in the middle of the screen, surrounded by all the objects detected around her, shown as fluorescent points with their distance.

Ships use radar to fix their position from landmarks or fixed references (islands, buoys) when navigating near the coast.

The IMO convention called COLREGs — the International Regulations for Preventing Collisions at Sea, issued in 1972 — requires that all ships maintain a radar lookout on board to obtain early warning of the risk of collision.

The GPS System

Satellite navigation
📖 2 sezioni
🎴 6 flashcard
❓ 3 domande esame
🛰️ Unit 8.8

Modern navigational methods use satellites instead of transmitting stations on the Earth. A dish-shaped aerial on the ship picks up the microwave radio signals sent out by the satellites. Each satellite constantly emits its own code signal and position; the GPS receiver picks up this message and calculates the time delay for the signal to arrive. Since we know the time and the speed of the signals (the speed of light, 300,000 km/s), it is easy to work out the distance from the satellite.

This operation is carried out simultaneously with three satellites and gives a geographical point in latitude and longitude. The position is extremely precise, with an error margin of only about 20 metres.

GPS stands for Global Positioning System. It uses a constellation of American satellites orbiting at about 20,000 km above the Earth, completing an orbit in 12 hours. The system was developed by the U.S. Defense Department (NAVSTAR GPS) and entered full operation in spring 1992. It is run from the Colorado Springs military base and currently consists of about 31 fully operational satellites. With this system we can fix our position at any time of day or night, anywhere on the Earth's surface.

Most GPS receivers do not just give the geographical position. They can calculate the speed, the course and any deviation from the original plotted course with extreme accuracy. They also register a large number of waypoints (punti di accostata), which allow the navigator to plot a route and carry out any adjustments to a chosen destination. Some models connect to other instruments on board, such as chart-readers or the navigational switchboard.

Ship's Crew & the Master

Organization · Ship's papers
📖 3 sezioni
🎴 7 flashcard
❓ 4 domande esame
🚢 Unit 9.1–9.2

The crew on a ship is divided into departments, the most important of which are the Deck Department and the Engine Department. A ship cannot operate with just deck officers or just engineers: the Engine Department ensures the safe running of the ship's machinery, while the Deck Department performs navigational duties, deals with the loading and unloading of cargo and communicates with shore stations and other ships.

Departments are organized in a hierarchy made up of Officers and Ratings. At the head of each department there is a First Officer, usually assisted in large ships by a lower-ranking Second and Third Officer. Ratings carry out tasks and execute the officers' orders; they are classified as Able or Ordinary Seamen (AS and OS).

At the top of all departments is the Master (or Captain), the highest authority on the ship. He has the overall responsibility for the ship, her cargo and the safety of the crew. All important operations are carried out with his consent and knowledge.

The Master is chosen by the shipowner to command the ship; he represents the shipowner in all circumstances. His job includes both technical and administrative functions: he must make sure the ship is seaworthy and properly equipped, that the cargo is loaded and stowed safely, and that the ship carries all the necessary documentation before leaving port.

Cargo handling is carried out either by the ship's own cargo gear or by shore cranes. Derricks are special ship's cranes, supported by masts or samson posts on the deck; they are operated by the ship's winches. Derricks, cranes and conveyor belts are commonly used for dry commodities. Many container ships are gearless (without cargo gear): they load and discharge their containers with the help of specialized port facilities. Most bulk carriers are also gearless. Roll-on/roll-off (RO/RO) ships load cargo over ramps through stern, bow or side ports, since they carry vehicles.

When a ship is going to leave port and get under way, she first has to get away from the dockside by unmooring all her lines. Mooring and unmooring operations are carried out by two deck teams — the Forward and Aft Mooring Stations — which communicate with the bridge using walkie-talkies.

The Captain first orders the stations to single up (cast off the double lines, leaving only single mooring lines). Then he gives the order to "let go forward" and "let go aft": the remaining single lines are cast off and pulled back aboard. When this is done, the stations report "all gone and clear". At the end of the procedure the Captain orders "Finished with manoeuvring stations!" and the teams go back on board.

Air Masses, Pressure & Winds

The moving air
📖 4 sezioni
🎴 7 flashcard
❓ 4 domande esame
🌬️ Unit 19

Meteorologists define an air mass as a part of the atmosphere where temperature and humidity are homogeneous. Air masses form when air remains for a long time over large, uniform areas of the Earth's surface that give it distinctive characteristics. The ideal conditions are found over continental areas and tropical deserts (warm and dry air masses) and over cold or warm oceanic areas (cold/warm, humid air masses).

Air masses keep their original characteristics for a long time. When they move into different regions they slowly modify and produce great movements of air around the globe.

The most accepted model today is the convection circulation of the air: warm air is lighter than cold air and tends to rise, while heavier cold air tends to descend under the force of gravity. The temperature varies between different geographical areas of the Earth, so the air pressure also varies.

Warm air weighs less (its molecules are more agitated and spread out) → low pressure. Cold air is heavier and rich with vegetation/moisture → high pressure. Lines joining points with the same atmospheric pressure are called isobars.

When a mass of warm air (low pressure) approaches a cold air mass (high pressure), it attracts air from the colder mass. This air, moving towards the area of lower pressure, causes an air current — namely the wind. The wind's intensity depends on how great the pressure difference is and how close the two masses are. In short, winds tend to restore a pressure equilibrium between adjacent zones by moving air from high to low pressure.

On the planet there are constant pressure zones, so air masses move in the same direction all year round, giving the main constant winds:

Clouds, Rain & Local Winds

Breezes · Beaufort · Monsoons
📖 4 sezioni
🎴 7 flashcard
❓ 3 domande esame
☁️ Handout

Clouds are formed from minute drops of water or small crystals of ice. Water vapour, always present in the air, condenses due to a drop in temperature: rising air meets colder layers, which favour condensation. For rain to happen, the temperature must drop enough to enlarge the droplets until they merge, hitting each other and attracting each other through electric charges, forming real drops of rain (1–5 mm) that fall.

Meteorologists classify clouds into three main types by their form (cirrus, stratus, cumulus) and by their height:

HeightCloudLook / weather
High (> 6000 m)Cirrusthin white filaments, made of ice crystals
Medium (2000–6000 m)Stratustransparent veil covering the sky
Low (< 2000 m)Cumulusthick round clusters, often fine weather
Low, greyNimbostratus / Cumulonimbus"nimb/nimbus" = rain, steady rain or thunderstorms

Local breezes are caused by the temperature differences between land and sea, because the ground and the water absorb the Sun's heat differently.

During the day, the ground warms up faster than the water; the air over the land is warmer and lighter (lower pressure), so it rises and is replaced by colder, heavier air coming from the sea → sea breeze (from sea to land).

At night, the Earth cools down faster than the sea, so the opposite happens: colder air from the land moves towards the sea → land breeze (from land to sea).

Wind speed is measured with an anemometer, which has a wheel that rotates in proportion to the wind. Speed is expressed in m/s or km/h, according to a scale worked out by the English Admiral Francis Beaufort in 1806. The Beaufort Scale ranges from 0 to 12: calm air is 0 and hurricanes are 12.

🇮🇹 Memo: brezza di mare = di giorno (dal mare alla terra); brezza di terra = di notte (dalla terra al mare). Anemometro misura il vento; scala Beaufort (1806) da 0 (calma) a 12 (uragano).

As well as constant winds, there are periodic winds, set up over oceans and continents by pressure differences that change with the seasons. Monsoons are periodic winds that alternate over six months, blowing from the sea to the land and from the land to the sea, caused by the high and low pressure zones that change between continents and oceans.

There are also variable winds (cyclones), local winds and land/sea breezes whose direction changes according to the local morphology.

Tides — Moon & Sun

Spring & neap tides
📖 2 sezioni
🎴 6 flashcard
❓ 3 domande esame
🌊 Moving waters

Tides are caused by the combined action of the attraction of the Moon and the centrifugal force of the Earth. On the side of the Earth turned towards the Moon, the sea swells under the Moon's influence and forms a high tide. The volume of water that forms this swelling is taken away from the surrounding zones, where there will be a low tide.

Surprisingly, a high tide also forms on the opposite side of the Earth (away from the Moon), because of the centrifugal force. So there are two high tides and two low tides as the Earth rotates.

Spring tides occur when the Moon is new or full: the Sun and the Moon are aligned (in line), and their forces add together, producing the highest high tides and the lowest low tides.

Neap tides occur when the Moon is in its first or last quarter: the Sun and the Moon are at right angles (perpendicular) to each other, so their forces partly cancel out, producing the weakest tides.

To make a routine call to another boat, first listen on the calling channel (Channel 16 or 09) for at least 30 seconds, to be sure you will not interfere with a conversation in progress. If the channel is clear, push down the talk button and call the name of the other boat in a normal tone:

"ANNIE — THIS IS QUEEN."

Always start with the name of the boat you are calling, then your own boat. Repeat the name two or three times if necessary; this first call should not exceed 30 seconds. When the other boat answers, it instructs you to switch to a working channel (e.g. Channel 68): "QUEEN, THIS IS ANNIE. REPLY 68". "Roger" means "I received your last call OK". Keep the message short (no more than 3 minutes; chit-chat is not permitted), then both boats sign off: "QUEEN — OUT." / "ANNIE — OUT."

The IMO (International Maritime Organization) is the specialized agency of the United Nations responsible for the safety and security of shipping and the prevention of marine and atmospheric pollution from ships. It was established in 1948 (entering into force in 1958) and has its headquarters in London. Its motto sums up its mission: "Safe, secure and efficient shipping on clean oceans."

The IMO does not run ships: it creates the international conventions (legal rules) that flag states then adopt and enforce on their vessels.

SOLAS (Safety Of Life At Sea) is generally regarded as the most important of all international treaties concerning the safety of merchant ships. The first version was adopted in 1914, after the Titanic disaster (1912); the version in force today is SOLAS 1974. It sets minimum standards for the construction, equipment and operation of ships, organised in chapters covering fire protection, life-saving appliances (lifeboats, life rafts), radio communications (GMDSS), safety of navigation, and the carriage of dangerous goods.

MARPOL (from MARine POLlution) is the main international convention for the prevention of pollution from ships, both from operational activities and from accidents. It is administered by the IMO (International Maritime Organization), the agency of the United Nations responsible for the safety of shipping and the protection of the marine environment.

It is known as MARPOL 73/78: the original convention was adopted in 1973 and then modified by a Protocol of 1978; the combined instrument entered into force in 1983. Today it is the most important set of rules for keeping the sea clean.

📌 In sintesi: MARPOL = convenzione IMO/ONU per prevenire l'inquinamento del mare causato dalle navi. Sigla MARPOL 73/78 (1973 + Protocollo 1978, in vigore dal 1983).

Annex VI limits the air pollution from ships' exhaust gases — mainly SOx (sulphur oxides) and NOx (nitrogen oxides), which cause acid rain and harm human health.

UNIT 8

Navigational Aids & Instruments

1. The magnetic compass

The Earth behaves like a giant magnet, so the magnetic needle points to the magnetic north pole, which does not coincide with the geographic (true) north. The angle between them is the variation (or declination, d): it depends on the geographic position and changes over time, and its value is printed on the chart. It is East (+) if magnetic north is east of true north (added), West (−) if west (subtracted). On board, the ship's ferrous metals deflect the needle by a further angle, the deviation (δ), which depends on the ship's heading and is recorded in the deviation table for every 15° of heading. Correction chain: compass course + deviation = magnetic course; magnetic course + variation = true course (d + δ = total error).

2. The gyrocompass

The gyrocompass uses a fast-spinning gyroscope mounted on a gimbal suspension to indicate true north, exploiting the Earth's rotation. Unlike the magnetic compass, it is not affected by magnetism and is stable in rough seas, which is why it is the primary heading reference. Its two key properties are gyroscopic inertia (it keeps the spin axis fixed in space) and precession (a force applied to the axis makes it turn in a perpendicular plane).

3. The sextant

The sextant measures the altitude (the vertical angle) of a celestial body — sun, moon, planets or stars — above the visible horizon. With the measured altitude and the exact time, the navigator works out a position line, the basis of celestial (astronomical) navigation. Its main parts are the graduated arc, the index mirror, the horizon glass and the telescope.

4. Log, echo sounder & sonar

The log measures the ship's speed and distance through the water. The echo sounder measures the depth under the keel by sending a sound pulse to the seabed and timing its echo (sound travels at about 1,500 m/s in water, depending on pressure, temperature and salinity). The sonar works on the same principle but can also scan horizontally to detect underwater objects.

5. Radar & the COLREGs

The radar (RAdio Detection And Ranging) sends radio waves at the speed of light and, from the returning echo, measures the bearing and the range of targets, which are shown on the screen (PPI, Plan Position Indicator). The ARPA (Automatic Radar Plotting Aid) tracks targets automatically and computes CPA (Closest Point of Approach) and TCPA to prevent collisions. The COLREGs (1972) require a proper radar lookout: there is a risk of collision when the bearing of another vessel stays constant while the range decreases.

6. The GPS system

The GPS (Global Positioning System) is a satellite navigation system with three segments — space (the constellation of satellites), control (the ground master station) and user (the receiver). The receiver needs signals from at least 4 satellites to fix its three-dimensional position (latitude, longitude and altitude); the fourth corrects the receiver's clock error. Typical accuracy is about 10–20 m, improved to about one metre with DGPS (Differential GPS). It is part of the wider GNSS family (GPS, GLONASS, GALILEO, BeiDou).

UNIT 9

The Deck Department

1. The crew & the Master

The crew of a merchant ship is divided into two main departments, the Deck department and the Engine department, plus the catering staff. Each is organised in a hierarchy of officers (who hold certificates of competency) and ratings (the unlicensed personnel). At the top is the Master (the captain), the highest authority on board: he is responsible for the safety of ship, cargo and crew, represents the shipowner and must ensure the vessel is seaworthy. The ship must carry all the ship's papers, such as the Certificate of Registry, the Tonnage and Load Line Certificates, the Intact Stability Booklet, the Minimum Safe Manning Document and the Crew List.

2. Officers & ratings

The deck officers are the Chief Officer (or First Mate, second in command, in charge of cargo operations and stability), the Second Officer (responsible for navigation and keeping the charts up to date), the Third Officer (responsible for the life-saving and fire-fighting equipment) and the Deck Cadet (a trainee officer). Among the ratings, the Bosun (boatswain) is the head of the deck ratings and supervises the Able Seamen and Ordinary Seamen in maintenance work, steering and lookout duties. Officers keep watches (the bridge is never left unattended), traditionally in four-hour periods.

3. Cargo handling & mooring

Cargo can be handled by the ship's own gear (derricks, cranes, winches) or by shore cranes; vessels without their own gear are called gearless, and RO/RO ships use ramps for rolling cargo. Mooring is the operation of securing the ship to the quay with ropes (head lines, stern lines, springs and breast lines) led through fairleads and made fast to bollards and bitts. When leaving, the typical orders are: "single up" → "let go forward/aft" → "all gone and clear" → finally "Finished with manoeuvring stations".

METEO

Meteorology, Winds & Tides

1. Air masses, pressure & winds

An air mass is a large body of air that is homogeneous in temperature and humidity, taking its characteristics from the region where it forms (polar/tropical, maritime/continental). Warm air rises and creates low pressure (a depression/cyclone); cold air descends and creates high pressure (an anticyclone). Wind is air moving from high to low pressure; because of the Earth's rotation the Coriolis force deflects it to the right in the Northern Hemisphere and to the left in the Southern. The main planetary winds are the trade winds, the westerlies and the polar easterlies. Where warm and cold air masses meet there is a front (warm, cold or occluded), which brings a change of weather.

2. Clouds, local winds & the Beaufort scale

Clouds form when humid air rises, cools and the water vapour condenses. They are classified by height and shape: cirrus (high, wispy), stratus (low, layered) and cumulus (puffy, vertical); the prefix/suffix "nimbus" indicates rain clouds (e.g. cumulonimbus = thunderstorms). Local breezes are caused by the different heating of land and sea: the sea breeze blows from sea to land by day, the land breeze from land to sea by night. Monsoons are seasonal winds. Wind speed is measured with an anemometer and described by the Beaufort scale (force 0 = calm to force 12 = hurricane).

3. Tides — Moon & Sun

Tides are the periodic rise and fall of the sea level, caused mainly by the Moon's gravitational attraction and, to a lesser extent, the Sun's, combined with the Earth's centrifugal force. In most places this produces two high tides and two low tides a day. When Sun, Earth and Moon are aligned (new and full Moon) the effects add up and we have the highest spring tides; when the Moon is at the quarters (90°) the effects partly cancel and we have the smaller neap tides. The vertical distance between high and low water is the tidal range; the horizontal movement of water is the tidal current, with slack water at the turn of the tide.

NAV & COMMS

Navigation & Communications

1. The dead reckoning plot

Dead reckoning (DR) is the method of estimating the ship's present position starting from the last known position (the last fix) and applying the course steered, the speed and the time elapsed. The estimated position must then be corrected for the effect of external forces: the drift is the displacement caused by the current, while the leeway is the sideways movement caused by the wind. A fix is a confirmed position obtained from independent references (bearings, radar ranges, GPS). On the chart the navigator draws the planned track, marks DR positions and updates them whenever a new fix is available.

2. VHF communications

VHF radio is used for short-range communication; Channel 16 is the international calling and distress frequency, while Channel 70 is used for DSC (Digital Selective Calling). There are three priority signals, each spoken three times: MAYDAY for distress (grave and imminent danger to life), PAN-PAN for urgency, and SÉCURITÉ for safety messages (navigational or weather warnings). A MAYDAY call gives, in order: the identity of the vessel, the position, the nature of the emergency, the assistance required, the number of people on board and any other useful information such as a description of the vessel.
